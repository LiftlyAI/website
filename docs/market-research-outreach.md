# Market Research — Lifter & Coach Outreach (IG DMs)

**Prepared:** 2026-06-17
**Method:** Cold Instagram DMs to strong lifters, plus one paid-coach interview. ~8 lifter threads, 1 coach. Window: May 30 – Jun 9, 2026.
**Sample caveat:** Small, qualitative, self-selected (people who replied to a cold DM). Directional signal, not statistically valid. Treat every "lifters do X" below as "the lifters who answered said X."

---

## 1. Executive Summary

The outreach confirms three things Liftly's thesis rests on, and surfaces two risks.

Confirmed: (1) the powerlifting community is unusually open to cold contact — almost every DM got a warm reply, which is a cheap acquisition channel; (2) the actual paying customer is the intermediate lifter chasing a round-number milestone, not the elite athlete; (3) a human remote coach is a thin manual stack — Google Sheets + WhatsApp video + RPE by feel for ~$100/mo — which is exactly the workflow Liftly automates.

Risks: (1) the strongest lifters mostly autoregulate by feel and don't follow a program, so they're not the market and shouldn't anchor the product; (2) both lifters and coaches treat form correction as individual judgment ("different for everyone"), not a fixed checklist — a caveat for pass/fail CV form-checks.

**Decision this supports:** keep building for the intermediate "get me to a 1000 total" lifter, position against the $100/mo Sheets-and-WhatsApp coach on cost and turnaround, and frame the CV form-check as individualized feedback rather than a rules grader.

## 2. Key Findings

### Finding 1 — Strong lifters reply to cold DMs
Nearly every opener got a friendly response: "ask away," "Happy to answer some questions," "Yeah of course ask anything you'd like." Several engaged in multi-message back-and-forth with a stranger.

- *Fact:* high reply rate to an unsolicited "I saw your video, can I ask about your program" message.
- *Inference:* the community runs on shared progress and is approachable; influencer/peer outreach is a viable, low-cost top-of-funnel.

### Finding 2 — The top lifters don't run a program; they autoregulate
One strong lifter: "I'm actually not on a program aside from often I run Smolov Jr bench and squat… training twice a week, volume and heavy, best accessories power squats, rack/block pulls and pull ups." The standard follow-up question — "do you change the weights based on how you're feeling for the day?" — assumes by-feel adjustment is the norm, and nobody pushed back on it.

- *Fact:* elite respondents train by feel with loose structure, not a prescribed plan.
- *Inference:* autoregulation (RPE / readiness) is the real mechanism at the top — validates Liftly reading fatigue and adapting load. But it also means the strongest lifters trust their own intuition and are *not* the buyer.

### Finding 3 — The buyer is the intermediate chasing a milestone
The person doing the asking states the goal plainly: "I just want my 3 lifts to total 1000." They copy strong lifters ("tryna get like u"), lack their own structure, and look for a plan to follow.

- *Fact:* stated goal is a round milestone total.
- *Inference:* this aspirational, under-structured intermediate is the ICP — has a clear number, no system to reach it, and actively seeks guidance.

### Finding 4 — The human-coach stack is thin, manual, and ~$100/mo
Sam (paid coach) described the whole operation: "I charge $100 a month. I use sheets because it works best for me and I get them to send me their vids on WhatsApp." Weekly loop = watch videos, read RPE, rebuild the sheet, send it back.

- *Fact:* competitor/substitute is Sheets + WhatsApp video + RPE, ~$100/mo, weekly turnaround, manual.
- *Inference:* Liftly automates this exact loop. The price point ($100/mo human) anchors willingness-to-pay and frames Liftly's pricing as the cheaper, faster alternative.

### Finding 5 — Coaching value is goal negotiation, not just programming
Sam, on what the job actually is: "a lot of personal preference into their program… some people wanna have a sick physique so powerbuilding, some wanna focus more on bench… we also talk about how their side goals can affect their main goal."

- *Fact:* coaches spend real effort reconciling competing goals (strength vs. physique vs. lift priority).
- *Inference:* the defensible, hard-to-automate part of coaching is goal trade-off conversation, not load math. A product that only prescribes weights leaves this gap open.

### Finding 6 — Form correction is treated as judgment, not a checklist
Asked whether form review gets repetitive, Sam: "no one's form is perfect… in the sense of looking at form and correcting, yes. But there's plenty of ways of coming at it so it's different for everyone."

- *Fact:* coaches resist framing form review as a fixed, repeatable rule set.
- *Inference:* a CV form-check sold as pass/fail will read as naive to this audience; framing it as individualized, prioritized feedback fits how the community thinks.

### Finding 7 — The "coach in bio" funnel is the incumbent acquisition channel
One coached athlete (275 paused bench, 600 deadlift, ~500 squat) credited rapid gains to "my coach in bio — he does all my programming," started ~6 weeks prior.

- *Fact:* sponsored strong athletes funnel followers to a coach linked in bio.
- *Inference:* this is the standard customer-acquisition path in the space; Liftly either competes with it or plugs into it (athletes/coaches as referral partners).

### Finding 8 — IG strength ≠ coaching credibility
The ex-competitor deflected the premise: "I'm definitely not the foremost example you could follow… 8 years competing, then a back surgery (military, unrelated to lifting) ended it. Just put in the work and stay consistent."

- *Fact:* a strong lifter explicitly disclaimed being a model to copy.
- *Inference:* social proof in this market is fuzzy — being strong on camera doesn't mean being a good coach, and some lifters know it. Room for a product that earns trust on results/feedback quality rather than physique.

## 3. Implications for Liftly

| Finding | What Liftly should do |
|---|---|
| Community replies to cold contact | Lean on peer/influencer outreach and referrals as a cheap top-of-funnel; this is already shown to work manually. |
| Elites autoregulate by feel | Keep readiness/RPE-driven adaptation central — it mirrors how the best train — but don't optimize the product for elite users. |
| Buyer = milestone-chasing intermediate | Make goal-setting concrete ("reach a 1000 total") the onboarding spine; meet them where the stated need is. |
| $100/mo manual coach | Position on price + turnaround: instant adaptation vs. a weekly sheet, well under $100. |
| Coaching = goal negotiation | Surface trade-offs (strength vs. physique vs. lift focus) in-product, not just prescribe load. This is the moat the human coach holds. |
| Form is judgment, not pass/fail | Frame the CV form-check as prioritized, individualized cues, not a grade. |
| "Coach in bio" funnel | Recruit strong athletes / small coaches as referral or B2B-console partners rather than only competing head-on. |

## 4. Risks & Caveats

- **Sample is tiny and self-selected.** ~8 lifters and one coach who chose to answer a cold DM. Don't generalize hard numbers from it.
- **One coach interviewed.** Sam's $100/mo Sheets+WhatsApp stack is a single data point on the competitor side. Confirm with more coaches before treating it as the market norm.
- **Substance was thin.** Several threads were only openers and "ask away" — engagement without content. The real signal comes from ~3 lifters and Sam.
- **Asker bias.** The questions assumed autoregulation ("change weights based on how you feel"), which may have steered answers toward confirming Liftly's thesis. Watch for leading questions in future outreach.
- **Elite ≠ buyer confusion.** The most quotable responses came from strong lifters who are *not* the target customer. Don't let their by-feel, no-program style shape a product meant for structured intermediates.

---

## 5. Recommendation

Proceed with the current direction, with sharpened positioning:

1. **Target the intermediate milestone-chaser**, not the elite. Make a concrete total goal the center of onboarding.
2. **Position explicitly against the $100/mo manual coach** — same loop (video + RPE + program), instant instead of weekly, lower cost.
3. **Close the goal-negotiation gap** the human coach owns: let users express side goals (physique, lift priority) and show how they trade against the main goal.
4. **Reframe the CV form-check** as individualized, prioritized feedback rather than pass/fail.
5. **Build the partner/referral funnel** with strong athletes and small coaches, mirroring the "coach in bio" path that already drives acquisition.

Next research step: interview 3–5 more paid remote coaches (price, tooling, turnaround, what they find tedious) and 5–10 intermediate lifters (current program, why they'd pay, what they'd want auto-adjusted) using neutral, non-leading questions.
